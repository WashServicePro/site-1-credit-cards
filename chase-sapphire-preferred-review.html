Who Is This Card Best For?

✈️
The frequent traveler who wants flexibility
You book flights and hotels regularly — sometimes through Chase Travel for that 5x rate, sometimes directly with airlines. The 14+ transfer partners let you squeeze maximum value from your points when a sweet spot pops up on United, Hyatt, or British Airways.
🍽️
The diner who travels — or the traveler who eats out
Dining at 3x and streaming at 3x means your everyday spending builds serious points inventory even in slow travel months. If you spend $400/month on dining, you're earning 14,400+ dining points a year alone — worth ~$180 in Chase Travel.
💳
New-to-travel-rewards cardholders building their first ecosystem
Chase Ultimate Rewards is the most beginner-friendly premium points currency. The portal is clean, the partners are strong, and the $95 annual fee is low enough that the welcome bonus alone covers multiple years of the fee.

Who Should Consider Another Card?

⚠️
Cardholders who prefer cash back over travel points. The Sapphire Preferred's redemption value is best realized through travel. If you primarily want straightforward cash back or statement credits, a flat-rate cash back card will be simpler and often more rewarding for your spending patterns.
⚠️
Heavy spenders who want premium lounge access and credits. If you're spending $10,000+ on travel annually and want airport lounge access, an annual travel credit, and more robust insurance, the Chase Sapphire Reserve (at $550/year) is worth the step up — its $300 annual travel credit effectively cuts the net cost significantly.

Our Verdict

★★★★½ 4.5 / 5

The Chase Sapphire Preferred has held its position as the benchmark mid-tier travel card for a decade — and it still earns that title. The 60,000-point welcome bonus is straightforward: spend $4,000 in three months and collect points worth $750 through Chase Travel, or potentially more if you leverage transfer partners strategically. For a $95 annual fee, that's an immediate, substantial return.

The earning structure is genuinely useful. Three points per dollar on dining and streaming isn't a narrow, forgettable category — it's where most people actually spend money. Pair that with 5x on Chase portal travel and 2x on everything else travel-related, and you have a card that builds points across your real life, not just a theoretical ideal spending day. The 10% anniversary bonus is a quiet but meaningful perk that doesn't get enough attention: it rewards loyalty in a concrete, compounding way.

Where the Preferred falls short relative to premium competitors is in hard-dollar benefits. There's no lounge access, no annual travel credit, and no Global Entry fee reimbursement. These aren't surprises at $95 — but they're worth naming, particularly for travelers who've grown accustomed to the credit ecosystem on the Reserve. The insurance suite (trip cancellation, auto rental collision, baggage delay) is genuinely solid and delivers real value when you need it.

Bottom Line

At $95 a year with a 60,000-point bonus and one of the strongest transfer partner networks in the US, the Chase Sapphire Preferred remains the best entry point into premium travel rewards — and the card most people should get first.
